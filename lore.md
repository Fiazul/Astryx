# Astryx — Lore

> The world, the fleet, and the people of *Astryx*. This is the in-universe codex:
> who flies, what they fly, what they fly *for*, and what waits out in the dark.

## The world
Space is quiet, and mostly empty, and that is exactly the point. *Astryx* is the long
fall outward from home — no war to win, no menus to clear, nothing to keep but the
charts you make. You leave **Earth**, you point the nose at a glowing dot on the black,
and you go. The dot grows. It resolves. It becomes a sun. You plant a beacon, the
chart remembers it forever, and then you pick another dot.

Everything is measured from **Sol**, the still center the charts are drawn around.
Far enough out and the old numbers stop meaning anything; pilots learn to read the
sky in light-years and trust the glow over the gauge.

## The Survey
Nobody owns the dark, but somebody has to *map* it. **The Survey** is the old guild of
deep-space cartographers — the reason there are charts at all. They don't pay in
money; there's nothing out here to spend it on. They pay in **recognition**: reach a
star no beacon has touched, claim it, and your name goes on that light forever.

Rook flies for the Survey. Not because they ask much of him — they don't — but because
a blank chart is the only thing he's ever found worth filling.

## Rook
**Rook** is the pilot. He flies because the alternative is standing still, and he was
never any good at standing still. He doesn't say much. He launches from Earth in
whatever hull the station has ready and lets the quiet do the rest.

## The fleet
The fleet is *hers* — a line of hulls Rook can swap into at the station, each with her
own temper and her own way of eating distance. You don't own a ship in *Astryx*. You
borrow one, and she decides how the trip goes. The station keeps them all fuelled and
waiting; which one you take is only ever your mood, never your rank.

- **Lyra** — the starter. The hull they hand you first, the one you launch in. Honest,
  unfussy, forgiving of a clumsy hand on the stick. Everyone's first sky was Lyra's.

- **Stella** — the original. The OG: balanced, dependable, the ride that never lets
  you down and never shows off about it. The name once belonged to Lyra and was set
  free; now it's Stella's alone, and it fits her.

- **Raptor** — the dangerous one. She runs two faces and you flip between them on a
  switch: **Combat**, where she becomes a wall of fire, and **Warp**, where she tears
  a hole in the distance and crosses it before the light catches up. Her booster burns
  purple — you know her by the trail.

- **Vela** — the sleek FTL cruiser, built for one thing: speed. Her warp drive winds
  up slow and then *blazes*, crossing a whole system on a single charge. Her booster
  burns gold. Pilots talk about Vela the way they talk about weather they barely
  survived.

Every hull is heavy in the hand — they carry their speed. Point her into a turn and
she *banks* into it, arcing the way real mass moves; she doesn't snap. Near a big
enough star you'll feel the pull on the stick, and so will anything you fire.

## Beacons, and the Atlas
The way you *keep* a star is to reach it and **plant a beacon**. Fly close enough, hold
the claim, and a survey marker drops into its light — the star is yours, logged into
the **Star Atlas** under your name, with everything the Survey knows about it: its
spectral class, its mass, how far its light has been travelling to reach you.

The Atlas is the whole reward. It fills as you fly. Every claimed star is a line that
won't un-write itself, and the count climbs toward a **rank** — *Cadet*, *Surveyor*,
*Cartographer*, *Star-Marshal* — a title that says how much of the dark has your name
on it. Nothing is gated behind it; the rank isn't a key, it's a *record*. The reward
for mapping the sky is having mapped the sky.

## The way of flying
Flying in *Astryx* has weight to it. You aim with the eye, you lean on the throttle,
and the hull banks into her turns like she means them — momentum carries you wide, so
you learn to lead your curves. Push hard and she boosts; ease off and she settles.
Gravity is real out here: stars and worlds tug at your hull and bend your fire, gently
in the lanes and hard near the giants. The galaxy is too big to take all at once, so
the good pilots learn its rhythms:

- **The guide** — you are never lost. The chart always knows the next unclaimed light
  and keeps an arrow on it; follow the arrow and the distance counts down. Cycle it to
  aim elsewhere whenever you like.

- **Warp / FTL** — the long-haul gift. Vela and Raptor-in-Warp spool their drives up
  toward light-year cruise and then back down. While she's screaming across the void,
  the guns go quiet — there's no fighting at that speed, only arriving. As you fall
  toward your mark she eases *out* of warp on her own, so you arrive instead of blowing
  straight past into the dark.

- **Free-look** — take your eye off the road. Hold the look and the view drifts off
  the tail to drink in the sky; let go and it snaps back behind her where it belongs.

- **The approach** — the closer you fall toward a world, the gentler she gets, easing
  down so you can really *see* it. Point away and she's free again, and the dot
  shrinks back into the dark behind you.

## The deep catalog
Most stars are just suns — bright, ordinary, worth a beacon and a line in the Atlas.
But the Survey keeps a short list of the things it sends only its best pilots after,
out where the charts go thin:

- **Neutron stars** — a sun's whole mass crushed into a city-sized point, spinning fast
  enough to strobe. Tiny, blinding, blue-white, sweeping the dark with beams. Their
  gravity is a wall; getting close enough to claim one is a feat by itself.

- **The burning nebula** — a young star still wrapped in the cloud that made it, lighting
  its own birthplace from inside. Fly into the glow and the gas closes around you. The
  rarest line in any Atlas.

## Vortex
Out past the safe lanes, in the **Alien Zone**, the regular hostiles drift — and among
them, the thing they're afraid of. **Vortex** is an alien hull grown monstrous, scaled
up far past anything a person should fly, lit with a red-hot menace that the dark
can't swallow. He is the boss of the deep: the deep catalog is on the far side of him,
and when his name is on the sky, the whole sky knows it.

## Earth, and the dots
You start at **Earth**, the launch point, the place the whole map remembers as home.
From there it's all dots — pinpricks of glow that are stars, planets, the rare strange
light that turns out to be something else. None of them are solid little worlds you can
land on and walk. They are *glow*: emissive, burning, growing as you close, the way a
real far thing looks when it finally gets near. That's the whole feeling of *Astryx* —
the dot becomes a sun, you put your name on it, and then you go find the next one.
